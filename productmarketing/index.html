The Scope

Five lanes of PMM work, none of them in the job title

Product marketing is the connective tissue between product, sales, marketing, and the customer. Below is what that meant inside Passion.io for two years, including the launch of YouAI, the company's AI coach product.

Lane 01

Positioning & Messaging

Defined value props for the YouAI launch. Built the messaging framework that ran across landing pages, sales decks, ads, and email. Founded on customer language, not brand voice docs.

Lane 02

Lifecycle Revenue

Owned the revenue line that produced $2.44M across two years. BFCM record in 2024. Doubled rev per lead. Webinar follow-up sequences alone produced $1.1M.

Lane 03

Customer Research

Reviewed 20+ ICP discovery and sales calls. Extracted voice of customer language across pain, objections, desired outcomes, beliefs. Expanded sample via Reddit, native forums, and social.

Lane 04

Internal Systems

Built and maintained six core Notion databases (Painpoints, Objections, Benefits, Personas, Outcomes, Angles) plus Brand Index and Knowledge Index. The source of truth the team's messaging strategy ran on.

Cross-functional & Dogfooding

Built my own YouAI to feel the product from the customer's seat. Built my own app on Passion.io as part of onboarding. Worked with developers, sales, design, and the founders on launch positioning, sales pages, and post-webinar feedback loops.

Customer Research

How the YouAI personas got built.

Sat in on 20+ ICP discovery and sales calls. Took transcripts. Watched facial cues, pulled vocal patterns, sorted pain points by intensity, mapped objections to the moment they showed up in the funnel. Cross-checked the language against Reddit threads, niche forums, and creator-economy social.

Output: three customer avatars with quoted limiting beliefs, sorted by buying stage. Plus an ever-expanding YouAI Messaging Rules page that codified the do's and don'ts for every team member writing copy.

Methodology I'm happy to walk through on a call. The actual avatar docs and messaging rules page are internal Passion.io property.

Strategy · Positioning

The outcome promise that was too big to convert.

YouAI's original positioning told the ICP they could go from $100K to $10M with a single product. Cross-checked it against how Hormozi, King Kong, and the rest of the high-ticket world actually segment by revenue tier. Nobody was making that jump in one product. They were running smaller, segmented promises tied to the bracket the prospect was actually in.

Surfaced the gap. The team subdivided ICPs into revenue brackets ($100K to $300K, $300K to $1M, $1M to $3M, $3M+). Sub-$100K founders got downsold to Passion.io instead of disqualified. The change made it into a system after I left.

Internal Systems

The Notion stack the marketing team ran on.

Painpoints, Objections, Benefits, Personas, Desired Outcomes, and Angles. Each linked to the next. Painpoints fed Angles. Personas pulled Objections by funnel stage. Benefits surfaced as messaging only when paired with quantified evidence. Plus a Brand Index and a Knowledge Index for product-level facts.

The system was the source of truth every campaign got built off. Field-level rules, source citations on every entry, ICP-specific filtering. Built and maintained over two years.

No screenshots (no longer have access to the workspace). Happy to walk through the architecture and field rules on a call.

Dogfooding

YouAI from the customer's seat.

The Head of Marketing asked the team to spend serious time inside the product. I took the lead. Created my own coach avatar against a specific knowledge base, walked through the same onboarding the user would, audited which features the product team was over-pitching versus under-pitching relative to the actual experience.

Other team members built theirs after. The audit fed directly into the launch messaging.

Dogfooding

Pinayo.app. My own app on Passion.io.

Part of onboarding. Built a real app on the platform, in the same flow a customer would. The topic was teaching writing. The point was not to ship publicly. The point was to feel the friction, find the spots where positioning and onboarding could be tighter, and bring those notes back into the marketing work.
