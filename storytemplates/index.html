1. The map: which story for which audience

One sentence covers the whole lecture: the colder the audience, the longer the story; the warmer the audience, the shorter the story. Story length and structure should match what the buyer already knows and feels.

Eugene Schwartz's five awareness levels, which is the cleanest sorting tool we have, looks like this:

Awareness levelWhat they knowStory jobBest story lengthBest frameworks
Unaware No problem identified. Going about life. Wake them up. Make them feel a tension they didn't know they had. Long. 800 to 5,000+ words. Or a 20 to 60 minute VSL. Hero's Journey, Three-Act, Founder origin story, "From Humiliating Defeat to Triumphant Victory" (Garfinkel)
Problem-aware They feel the pain. Don't know solutions exist. Name the pain better than they can. Hint at a way out. Medium. 300 to 1,500 words. Pixar Story Spine, Kishōtenketsu, "Stories About Your Prospect's Pain" (Garfinkel), Customer-as-hero
Solution-aware They know solutions exist. Comparing. Differentiate. Show why this approach beats the others. Medium. Compressed. Mechanism-as-story, Kishōtenketsu (twist reframes the category), StoryBrand SB7
Product-aware They know your product. Not bought yet. Reduce risk. Reassure. Stack proof. Short. 150 to 600 words. Customer case-study story, "Reassurance Stories" (Garfinkel), one-paragraph testimonial-as-story
Most aware Brand-loyal. Just need a reason now. Trigger action. Move them today. One-liner to two-liner. Compressed anecdote, "Dissolver" objection-killer (Garfinkel), urgency micro-story

The non-obvious bit: at most-aware, a 30-minute hero's journey doesn't help, it actively annoys. The buyer is reaching for the wallet, not the popcorn. So you compress. The same plot beats are still there, just squeezed into one line. (Section 7 shows how.)

The four big jobs of a story (the way I'll order this lecture)

  1. Frameworks give you the spine: where the story turns and ends.
  2. Openings decide whether the story is read at all.
  3. Showing (vs. telling) decides whether the reader feels anything.
  4. Compression decides whether the story works for cold ads, LinkedIn posts, and email subject lines, not just sales pages.

2. Story frameworks (with diagrams)

Each framework below: the spine, the diagram, and where in the funnel I'd actually use it.

2.1 Hero's Journey (Joseph Campbell, simplified by Christopher Vogler to 12 stages)

Spine: ordinary life, disruption, refusal, mentor, threshold, tests, ordeal, reward, return transformed.

Use it for: UnawareProblem-aware long-form sales letters, VSLs, founder origin pages, brand films.

Skip it for: Most aware ad headlines, retargeting, transactional emails. The full arc is too slow.

2.2 Kishōtenketsu (Japanese 4-act, no conflict required)

Spine: set up, develop, twist with something seemingly unrelated, then resolve by showing how 1+2+3 connect.

Use it for: Problem-awareSolution-aware LinkedIn posts, mid-funnel emails, category-redefining pitches. It's the framework most copywriters underuse.

Why I love it for marketing: the "Ten" beat is exactly how good positioning works. You're walking the reader through what they expect, then introducing one apparently irrelevant fact, then revealing it changes the whole game.

2.3 Pixar Story Spine (Kenn Adams, 1991, popularized by Emma Coats)

Spine: Once upon a time ___. Every day ___. Until one day ___. Because of that ___. Because of that ___. Until finally ___. And ever since then ___.

Use it for: Problem-awareSolution-awareProduct-aware case studies, customer stories, founder origin in 200 words, email body, ad scripts, 60-second VSL openers. This is the most compressible long-form framework.

Why it travels: it survives compression. You can run a real Pixar Story Spine in 6 sentences on LinkedIn and still feel like a story.

2.4 Dan Harmon Story Circle (8 steps)

Spine: You. Need. Go. Search. Find. Take. Return. Change.

Use it for: UnawareProblem-awareSolution-aware webinars, founder talks, brand video, narrative case studies. It's the Hero's Journey on a diet, sized for a 22-minute sitcom or a 5-minute pitch.

Why this beats Hero's Journey for content marketing: 8 steps fit a podcast episode. 12 stages don't.

2.5 StoryBrand SB7 (Donald Miller)

Spine: a character with a problem meets a guide who gives them a plan that ends in success or failure.

Use it for: Solution-awareProduct-aware homepages, sales pages, pitch decks, "what we do" sections. It's a positioning framework dressed as a story.

Honest caveat: SB7 is positioning, not narrative. It will not write you a moving story. It will write you a clear one.

2.6 Three-Act Structure (the most underrated framework because it's the most basic)

Spine: setup, confrontation, resolution.

Use it for: everything that doesn't earn a fancier framework. All awareness levels Default to this when in doubt. Most "stories" in copy are really just three-act stories with a haircut.

2.7 Vonnegut's Shapes of Stories (the emotional arcs, not a framework, a design tool)

Vonnegut sketched stories as graphs of fortune over time. Researchers later confirmed almost every story uses one of six shapes. This isn't a framework you write to. It's a check you run on your draft: does my story have a shape, or is it flat? Flat stories don't sell.

For copy, the two highest-performing shapes are Man in a Hole (fall, then rise: most rags-to-riches case studies) and Cinderella (rise-fall-rise: most great founder stories, including Steve Jobs at Apple).

2.8 Garfinkel's Persuasion Story Code: 6 categories of selling stories

From David Garfinkel's The Persuasion Story Code. He covers 25 specific story types organized into six families. The families are what matter for this lecture; the specific 25 are templates.

FamilyWhat it doesAwareness fit
Origin StoriesBuild confidence. Show your background makes you the right person.Unaware → Solution-aware
Stories About Your Prospect's PainBuild trust. Show you understand them better than they understand themselves.Problem-aware → Solution-aware
Stories That Predict the FuturePaint a vivid after-state. The transformation is felt, not described.Solution-aware → Product-aware
Reassurance StoriesHandle doubts before they kill the sale.Product-aware → Most aware
Stories That ExplainPersuade while explaining. Mechanism wrapped in narrative.Solution-aware → Product-aware
Stories That Build TrustOrganize credentials, reviews, expert endorsements, case histories.Product-aware → Most aware

Garfinkel also teaches three "DNA" story patterns that show up in many of the most successful sales letters in history:

  1. From Humiliating Defeat to Triumphant Victory. Charles Atlas's "97-pound weakling" sales letter is the textbook case.
  2. Your Unrecognized Greatness Has Been Discovered. Reader feels seen for who they could become.
  3. The Secret Formula for Success and Prestige. Hidden knowledge, in your hands now.

Notice these are emotional arcs disguised as plots. Pattern 1 is "Man in a Hole." Pattern 2 is "Rags to Riches." Pattern 3 is "Cinderella." Vonnegut and Garfinkel rhyme.

3. Ten real examples, labeled

Same frameworks, different shapes and lengths. I've labeled the framework in use, the awareness level it was written for, and the opening type (covered in section 4).

Example 1 / Hero's Journey, Unaware audience, Vulnerable Confession opening

Brené Brown, The Power of Vulnerability, TED 2010. She opens with an event planner refusing to call her a researcher because "no one will come, they'll think you're boring and irrelevant." That's the threshold (her identity is challenged). She then walks through the ordeal of a research breakdown, her therapist visits, and her return with the elixir: a thesis on wholehearted living.

Source: Brené Brown TED Talk transcript, ted.com
Example 2 / Pixar Story Spine, Problem-aware audience, Anecdote opening

Susan Cain, The Power of Introverts, TED 2012. Opens with "When I was nine years old, I went off to summer camp for the first time. And my mother packed me a suitcase full of books." Once upon a time = nine-year-old Susan. Every day = books at home. Until one day = camp counselor demanded "rowdy" cheers. Because of that, because of that = years of self-suppression. Until finally = adult Susan reclaiming introversion. And ever since = the talk and the book.

Source: Susan Cain TED Talk transcript, ted.com
Example 3 / Hero's Journey + Origin Story, Unaware to Solution-aware, Grandmother Frame opening

Bryan Stevenson, We Need to Talk About an Injustice, TED 2012. Opens with his grandmother sitting him down at age eight or nine to ask him to "promise me three things." That's the mentor and the call wrapped together. The talk is structurally a Hero's Journey for a movement. The personal grandmother frame brings cold listeners to problem-aware in 90 seconds.

Source: Bryan Stevenson TED Talk transcript, ted.com
Example 4 / Cinderella arc, Founder Origin, Childhood Reframe opening

Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie, The Danger of a Single Story, TEDGlobal 2009. "I'm a storyteller. And I would like to tell you a few personal stories." She opens with herself, age seven, writing stories about white characters eating apples in the snow despite living in Nigeria and eating mangoes. Innocent rise, painful realization, mature reframe. Cinderella shape used to deliver an idea, not a product.

Source: Chimamanda Adichie TED Talk transcript, ted.com
Example 5 / Garfinkel "From Humiliating Defeat to Triumphant Victory", Unaware/Problem-aware, Public Shame opening

The Charles Atlas "97-pound weakling" ad (1940s, ran for decades). Opening: a skinny kid named "Joe" gets sand kicked in his face by a bully on the beach in front of his date. By panel six he's transformed and knocks the bully out. Same one-page comic-strip ad ran for forty years. This is Garfinkel's first DNA pattern, packed into 8 panels.

Source: Charles Atlas Dynamic Tension ads, widely reproduced
Example 6 / Stroke of Insight, Problem-aware, Backstory-First opening

Jill Bolte Taylor, My Stroke of Insight, TED 2008. Opens with: "I grew up to study the brain because I have a brother who has been diagnosed with a brain disorder, schizophrenia." Note the choice. She doesn't open with the stroke (the dramatic moment). She opens with the brother (the stake). The stroke comes after she's earned the right to talk about brains.

Source: Jill Bolte Taylor TED Talk transcript, ted.com
Example 7 / Three-Act, Solution-aware, In Medias Res opening

Most direct-mail control sales letters by Gary Halbert and David Deutsch open this way: "I was sitting in a hotel room in Phoenix when the phone rang. It was my wife. She was crying." We're dropped into act 2 already. Backstory follows. Resolution is the offer. Three-act, but the curtain rises mid-fight. This is the dominant copy structure for direct response, full stop.

Source: documented in copywriting swipe collections (Halbert, Deutsch)
Example 8 / Kishōtenketsu, Solution-aware to Product-aware, Stat opening

A LinkedIn post format that works: "The average SaaS founder writes 47 emails a day. (Ki) Most of them get ignored. (Shō) But the highest-performing reps I know write 8. (Ten, the twist, unrelated framing) Here's why volume is the wrong metric. (Ketsu, resolution: tying it back to the offer)" The Ten beat doesn't have to be unrelated content; it has to be an unexpected reframe. That's the move.

Source: pattern observed across LinkedIn solopreneur posts (Welsh, Valles, others)
Example 9 / Pixar Story Spine in 6 lines, Problem-aware, Justin Welsh-style

"In 2019, I was a burnt-out VP of Sales. (Once upon a time) Earning $400K and miserable. (Every day) Then I got laid off. (Until one day) Because of that, I built a one-person business. (Because of that) Because of that, I crossed $5M solo. (Because of that) Now I write to teach the path." Six lines, full Story Spine, no padding. Works because each beat earns its own line break on LinkedIn.

Source: pattern reproduced from Justin Welsh's "I wrote my first LinkedIn post" thread template
Example 10 / One-liner story, Most aware, Punchline opening

"Paid a guy $130 to move a treadmill. Took him 30 minutes." That's the entire story. There's a setup (paid this much), a turn (the treadmill detail), and an implication (unsexy businesses make money). The reader fills in the arc. This is what the squeeze looks like at the most-aware stage. Justin Welsh has built a multi-million dollar business posting variations of this.

Source: Justin Welsh LinkedIn, summarized in profile coverage

4. Story openings (named, with real examples)

The opening decides whether the story is read at all. The names below are self-explanatory. Each pattern works with any of the frameworks above. You can run a Hero's Journey that opens with a Vulnerable Confession, or a Pixar Story Spine that opens In Medias Res. The opening is independent of the spine.

The "% of story" tag is a rough share of the total piece this opening should occupy.

4.1 Vulnerable Confession

5 to 10% of the total story

What it does: opens with something the speaker is embarrassed about, ashamed of, or genuinely worried to admit. Disarms reader skepticism instantly.

"So, I'll start with this: a couple years ago, an event planner called me because I was going to do a speaking event. And she called, and she said, 'I'm really struggling with how to write about you on the little flyer.'"
Brené Brown, The Power of Vulnerability

Why it works: the audience can't argue with a confession. Status drops, attention rises.

Best for: Unaware, Problem-aware, founder bio, About pages.

4.2 In Medias Res (drop into peak action)

10 to 15% of the total story

What it does: opens at the most kinetic moment. Skips exposition. The reader spends the rest of the piece working out how we got here.

"I was sitting in a hotel room in Phoenix when the phone rang. It was my wife. She was crying."
Direct-mail sales letter convention (Halbert, Deutsch, and many others)
"Tyler gets me a job as a waiter, after that Tyler's pushing a gun in my mouth and saying the first step to eternal life is you have to die."
Chuck Palahniuk, Fight Club, opening lines

Why it works: the brain hates unanswered questions. You manufacture three on the first sentence.

Best for: all awareness levels, especially when the audience is bored or distracted.

4.3 The Childhood Anchor

10 to 20% of the total story

What it does: opens with a specific scene from speaker's childhood. Buys the speaker permission to talk about identity and values.

"When I was nine years old, I went off to summer camp for the first time. And my mother packed me a suitcase full of books, which to me seemed like a perfectly natural thing to do."
Susan Cain, The Power of Introverts
"I'm a storyteller. And I would like to tell you a few personal stories about what I like to call 'the danger of the single story.' I grew up on a university campus in eastern Nigeria."
Chimamanda Adichie, The Danger of a Single Story

Why it works: a child cannot be held responsible. Defenses drop.

Best for: Unaware, Problem-aware, brand films, founder origin.

4.4 The Single Question

2 to 5% of the total story

What it does: a single short question reframes the whole talk before the story begins.

"Why are some people and organizations more innovative, more influential, and more profitable than others?"
Simon Sinek, How Great Leaders Inspire Action

Why it works: the reader's mind is now hunting for an answer in everything that follows.

Best for: all awareness levels. Especially good as a sub-headline that introduces a story underneath.

4.5 The Stat That Doesn't Compute

3 to 8% of the total story

What it does: opens with a number so wrong-feeling the reader has to know why.

"For every dollar an American company spends on a Black male, it spends about 60 cents on a Black female. The criminal justice system is just one part of that broken story."
pattern from Bryan Stevenson and others; not a direct quote

Why it works: the gap between the number and what the reader assumed creates curiosity.

Best for: Problem-aware, Solution-aware. Pair with a story that explains the stat.

4.6 The Backstage Reveal

8 to 12% of the total story

What it does: opens by telling the reader something they weren't supposed to know about the speaker's industry, company, or self.

"Most of my time is spent in jails, in prisons and on death row. Most of my time is spent in very low-income communities, in the projects, and places where there's a great deal of hopelessness."
Bryan Stevenson, We Need to Talk About an Injustice

Why it works: the reader feels chosen. Insider access is high-status content.

Best for: Problem-aware, Solution-aware, expert positioning, B2B content.

4.7 The Stake Before the Story

5 to 10% of the total story

What it does: names what the speaker has personally lost or risked, before telling the story that earned that loss.

"I grew up to study the brain because I have a brother who has been diagnosed with a brain disorder, schizophrenia."
Jill Bolte Taylor, My Stroke of Insight

Why it works: stakes earn attention. We don't care about a story until we know what's at risk.

Best for: Unaware, Problem-aware, mission-driven founders, anything emotional.

4.8 The Joke (or False-Start Joke)

3 to 8% of the total story

What it does: opens with a long-form joke that demonstrates storytelling chops without claiming to.

Andrew Stanton (Pixar, The Clues to a Great Story, TED 2012) opens with an extended off-color bar joke. Once the audience is laughing, he reveals: that joke is a story, and storytelling is "joke-telling."

Why it works: a laugh is permission for the next 12 minutes.

Best for: Solution-aware, Product-aware, talks, webinars, podcasts. Not for sales pages (no laughs above the fold).

4.9 The Direct Address

2 to 5% of the total story

What it does: drops the wall and talks straight to the reader before any story begins.

"If you're reading this, you're probably tired. Not the kind of tired sleep fixes."
common LinkedIn opener, used by Justin Welsh and many others

Why it works: a story about the reader is the only story they can't ignore.

Best for: Problem-aware, Solution-aware, social posts, cold outbound.

4.10 The Punchline First

10 to 30% of the total story (the entire story, in some cases)

What it does: tells the conclusion of the story in line one. Backfills only enough to earn it.

"Paid a guy $130 to move a treadmill. Took him 30 minutes."
Justin Welsh, LinkedIn

Why it works: at most-aware, the buyer doesn't need a journey, they need a fact dressed as a story.

Best for: Most aware, retargeting ads, social posts, email subject lines.

5. Show don't tell, learned from fiction writers (not copywriters)

Most copywriters teach show-don't-tell at the level of a Pinterest quote. The fiction writers go deeper. Four sources are enough.

5.1 Anton Chekhov, in a letter to his brother (1886)

"In descriptions of Nature one must seize on small details, grouping them so that when the reader closes his eyes he gets a picture. For instance, you'll have a moonlit night if you write that on the mill dam a piece of glass from a broken bottle glittered like a bright little star, and that the black shadow of a dog or a wolf rolled past like a ball."
Anton Chekhov

The famous shortened paraphrase is "Don't tell me the moon is shining; show me the glint of light on broken glass." Chekhov never actually wrote the short version. He wrote the long version, which is more useful: describe small concrete details, grouped, so the reader assembles the abstract idea themselves.

5.2 Chuck Palahniuk's "thought verbs" rule

From his essay Nuts and Bolts: 'Thought' Verbs (LitReactor). Palahniuk gives writers one assignment: for the next six months, do not use any "thought verb" in your writing. The banned list: thinks, knows, understands, realizes, believes, wants, remembers, imagines, desires, loves, hates.

His instruction:

"Thinking is abstract. Knowing and believing are intangible. Your story will always be stronger if you just show the physical actions and details of your characters and allow your reader to do the thinking and knowing. And loving and hating."
Chuck Palahniuk

Telling (with thought verbs)

"Sarah hated her job. She knew she had to quit. She believed there was something better out there for her."

Showing (Palahniuk's rule applied)

"Sarah opened her laptop at 8:47 a.m. The blue glow lit up her face. She read the first three emails, then closed the lid. Pushed the chair back. Stood up. Walked into her boss's office without knocking."

5.3 Show emotion through behavior, not adjectives

Angela Ackerman and Becca Puglisi's Emotion Thesaurus catalogs over 130 emotions and the physical signs they produce. The principle for copy is: name the symptom, not the emotion.

If a character is angry, don't write "she was angry." Write the symptoms an outside observer would see two feet away:

  • jaw tightening, then unclenching
  • knuckles whitening on the steering wheel
  • silence held a beat too long
  • a polite voice, several degrees colder than her usual one
  • the way she folded a napkin, slowly, into smaller and smaller squares

For fear: face turning ashen, hair lifting on the nape, clammy hands, a swallow that takes too long. For sadness: drooping eyelids, downcast eyes, corners of the mouth pulling down, eyebrows slanting down from the middle. The face literally falls.

5.4 Specificity beats summary, every single time

"He drove a car" tells you nothing. "He drove a beige '97 Corolla with a cracked side mirror held on by duct tape" tells you everything: socioeconomics, character, era, the kind of week he's having.

Vague

"The office was busy and stressful."

Specific

"Three Slack notifications in two minutes. The microwave beeped four times before anyone got up. Someone laughed too loud near the printer."

The vague version asks the reader to feel busy. The specific version makes them feel busy.

6. Sensory and emotional language

6.1 The five senses, and how copy uses them

SenseWhat it's good for in copyExample phrase
SightSetting, status, character, "the room""He walked in wearing a watch that cost more than her car."
SoundTension, presence, intimacy"You could hear the freezer hum from the next room. That's how quiet she got."
TouchPhysical state, body, weather"The phone in his pocket buzzed against his thigh, then again, then again."
SmellMemory, place, time of year"The hallway smelled like microwaved fish and printer toner."
TasteThe most underused sense in copy. Use it for poverty, wealth, illness, surprise."The coffee was burned. He drank it anyway."

Rule of three: a single sense feels flat. Three senses in a paragraph feel real. Don't go above three or you sound like a perfume ad.

6.2 Emotional arcs in copy

Vonnegut's lesson, applied to a sales page: every section should move the reader's emotion in a specific direction. If you can't draw a line graph of how your reader feels from headline to call-to-action, your story doesn't have a shape.

For most direct response, the working shape is Man in a Hole: the reader is in the hole at the start (problem), the middle is the ladder out (solution), the end is the elixir (offer). If your draft doesn't follow that or one of Vonnegut's other five shapes, it's flat.

6.3 Subtext and the "iceberg" principle

Hemingway's iceberg theory: the reader should feel that 90% of the story is underwater, even though only 10% is on the page. In short-form direct response this matters more than in long-form. A LinkedIn one-liner story works exactly because it implies a much bigger story the reader is invited to fill in.

"Paid a guy $130 to move a treadmill. Took him 30 minutes." The story underneath, that the reader fills in, is: most "real" businesses are simple, undignified, and overlooked.

7. The squeeze: long stories compressed for LinkedIn and ads

This is the section most copywriting books skip. The same Hero's Journey or Pixar Story Spine that fills 5,000 words on a sales page can be compressed to 60 words for LinkedIn or 12 words for an ad. The frameworks survive the squeeze. The padding doesn't.

7.1 The Pixar Story Spine, compressed

LengthFormatExample shape
3,000 wordsLong-form sales pageEach Spine beat gets its own section. "Until one day" is the headline of the page.
600 wordsEmail, LinkedIn long-postOne paragraph per Spine beat. 7 paragraphs total.
60 wordsLinkedIn standard postOne short line per Spine beat. Line breaks for breathing room.
12 wordsAd headline, email subjectSkip beats 1, 2, 5, 6, 7. Keep beats 3 ("until one day") and 4 ("because of that"). Implication does the rest.
1 linePunchline-first social postHeadline beat 6 ("until finally"). Reader infers everything else.

7.2 The Hero's Journey, compressed

You can't ship a 12-stage Hero's Journey on LinkedIn. You can ship the four load-bearing stages: Ordinary World, Call, Ordeal, Return. Everything else is decoration. Compression of the journey looks like this:

"For ten years I sold software. (Ordinary World) Then I got fired during the 2020 round. (Call) The next 14 months were the worst of my life. (Ordeal) I came out of it running my own agency, and I will never go back. (Return)"

That's a compressed Hero's Journey in 4 sentences. The full 12-stage version is what the founder tells when someone DMs them after reading the post.

7.3 The one-line story

A real one-line story has three things, even if only one is on the page:

  1. A specific image the reader can see. ("a guy moving a treadmill")
  2. A number, name, or detail that locks it into reality. ("$130", "30 minutes")
  3. An implied turn the reader completes for themselves. (the unsexy job paid this much for this little)

Not a one-line story (a slogan in disguise)

"Sometimes the best businesses are the ones nobody wants to start."

A one-line story

"Paid a guy $130 to move a treadmill. Took him 30 minutes."

7.4 The two-line story (the workhorse)

Two lines is enough room to set up an expectation in line 1 and break it in line 2. This is the most reliable LinkedIn unit.

"My best client this year doesn't have a website. He just answers his phone."
"I was hired to write 12 emails. I sent the first one. The campaign hit revenue target before I wrote the next 11."

Both run on a Three-Act spine. Setup, turn, implied resolution.

7.5 The compressed Kishōtenketsu (4 lines, ~50 words)

Kishōtenketsu compresses better than any other framework because the "Ten" beat is a hard pivot the reader can feel even in 12 words.

"Most consultants list 47 services on their site. (Ki)
Most don't get hired. (Shō)
The ones who do, list one. (Ten, the twist)
The shortest menu wins the hungry buyer. (Ketsu)"

8. When to tell which story

Five story types every direct-response writer should be fluent in. For each one: when to use it, when not to.

8.1 The founder origin story

Use when: selling against incumbents; the buyer is choosing a person, not a product; the brand is new and trust hasn't been built; the founder has a real, specific reason to have built the thing.

Skip when: the buyer is most-aware and just needs the offer; the founder's story has nothing to do with the buyer's problem; the founder is famous enough that the story is already known.

Awareness fit: Unaware, Problem-aware, Solution-aware.

Anti-pattern: "I started this company because I'm passionate about X." That's not a story. That's a slogan.

8.2 The customer story (case study as narrative, not as data dump)

Use when: the buyer is comparing options; trust needs to come from a peer, not from you; the result is unusual or counterintuitive.

Skip when: the customer's situation is too different from the buyer's; the result was driven by factors the buyer can't replicate.

Awareness fit: Solution-aware, Product-aware.

Best framework: Pixar Story Spine. Once upon a time, the customer had a problem. Every day, they tried things that didn't work. Until one day, they tried this. Because of that, X. Because of that, Y. Until finally, Z. And ever since.

8.3 Mechanism-of-action wrapped in a story

Use when: your product works in a way that's non-obvious or hard to believe; the category is crowded and you need to show why your thing is different on a level deeper than features.

Skip when: the buyer doesn't care about how it works, only about the outcome (most consumer products at most-aware).

Awareness fit: Solution-aware, Product-aware.

How to wrap it: the mechanism becomes the protagonist. "There's a single nerve in the lower back that's responsible for 80% of chronic pain. Most treatments hit the wrong nerve. Here's the moment one doctor realized that, and what happened next." The mechanism is taught while a story is told. Reader doesn't notice they're being educated.

8.4 The objection-killer story (Garfinkel's "Dissolver")

Use when: the buyer is product-aware but not buying because of one specific objection; the objection is common and predictable.

Skip when: you don't actually know which objection is blocking the sale.

Awareness fit: Product-aware, Most-aware.

How it works: name a customer who had the same objection, walk them through how the objection turned out to be wrong, end on the result. Two paragraphs. No moralizing.

8.5 The contrarian one-liner story (Most-aware, repeat buyers, social)

Use when: the buyer already knows you, the platform punishes long posts, you have a strong opinion that needs evidence.

Skip when: you're trying to introduce yourself or your category. One-liners assume context.

Awareness fit: Most-aware.

9.1 Pre-flight checks before shipping any story

  1. Spine: can I name the framework I used? If no, the story doesn't have a spine, it has filler.
  2. Shape: can I draw a Vonnegut emotional line graph of this piece? If it's flat, rewrite.
  3. Show, not tell: circle every "thought verb" (thinks, knows, believes, feels, wants). Replace 80% of them with physical actions or sensory detail.
  4. Specificity: circle every adjective. Replace generic ones (busy, happy, big, small) with concrete details.
  5. Three senses: at least one paragraph in any story over 200 words should hit three of the five senses.
  6. Awareness fit: does the length match the awareness level? If I'm writing 2000 words for a most-aware buyer, I'm losing them.
  7. Opening: does the first sentence raise a question the rest of the piece answers? If not, rewrite the first sentence.
